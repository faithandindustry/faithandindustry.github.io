Yasmin Kuymizakis aka JOON (Italians Do It Better) is Ġiżimina. Working across performance and sound installation with Maltese folk music, cultural memory, and reinterpretation. Id-Dlam Id-Dawl (The Darkness The Light) is a seven-track album built from archive folk guitar recordings. Reworking sampled fragments of Maltese musical memory into something intimate and personal. The album moves through change, loss, uncertainty, and renewal, sitting between darkness and light as states of transformation.

Faith And Industry has always had an ear for outer folk experimentation and here we present the first release in Maltese language. Uniquely the only Semitic language written in Latin script, evolved from Siculo-Arabic and spoken by around 570,000 people across the diasporic communities.

An accomplished electronic musician Ġiżimina’s (Yasmin) production and composition experience takes this to a wider audience beyond. Having Toured throughout North America and Europe supporting Future Islands (2023–2024). Composed and produced original music for the video game “Wheel World” (2025), and created music, sound design and mixing work across film, theatre and fashion, including “Dolce”, a film for the Venice Biennale directed by Charlie Cauchi (2026).

The guitar recordings where captured on cassette tape between 1980-90 in bars and garages where the music was performed. Ġiżimina embarked on the making of this collection following a live show aside filmmaker and sound collector Vincent Moon in 2025..

Cavallini/Nada — Continuum

FAI037 • Ambient / Electronic / Experimental

Continuum is the debut album from sound healer Giulia Cavallini and electronic producer Karlos Nada, and it arrives as something genuinely rare: a record that feels unhurried, unforced, and completely at ease with itself. It is the sound of two friends who have spent years listening deeply, both to music and to one another, finally deciding to make something together.

Giulia Cavallini works at the intersection of sound, the body, and presence. As a practitioner of sound healing, she brings gongs and singing bowls not merely as instruments but as vessels for vibration, tools shaped by centuries of contemplative tradition. She is a member of the Planetary Gong Ensemble and an active performer across multiple musical contexts. Through “Presence” she explores what it means to be fully attentive as a musician, treating live performance as a meditative discipline. Through “Hand of Glory” she moves into darker, more ritualistic sonic territory. Across all of these endeavours a consistent thread runs through her work: sound as a means of transformation, whether experienced alone in a quiet room or shared with a crowd in real time.

Karlos Nada is an electronic producer with a restless curiosity and a highly developed ear for space and texture. His work under his own name draws on the fringes of ambient music and electronic composition, while “Join the Din” explores more expansive, beat driven territory. What distinguishes him as a producer is a commitment to craft: to the patient shaping of sonic environments where every element earns its place. His productions tend to breathe, to leave room, and to resist the impulse toward unnecessary decoration.

When Giulia and Karlos began working on what would become Continuum, there was no grand conceptual framework driving the sessions. There was simply trust, shared curiosity, and a long established friendship that made experimentation feel natural. Giulia's gongs and bowls provide the living, breathing foundation of the record, their overtones blooming and decaying in real time. Around and within these acoustic resonances, Karlos built an electronic landscape that responds and reflects rather than competes: subtle rhythmic patterns that feel like breath, glowing drones, and delicate IDM influenced textures that emerge and dissolve at the edges of perception.

The album also features the flute of Jin Theriault, whose contributions add a further dimension of organic warmth. Where the gongs carry mass and the electronics carry structure, the flute carries something more elusive: a voice tracing the contours of the sound around it and finding something worth following.

Continuum is designed to be heard in one uninterrupted sitting, as a continuous movement rather than a collection of individual pieces. The music does not push or insist; it creates conditions, a slower pace, a widening of awareness, an invitation to follow the arc of sound the way one might follow the arc of a long, slow breath. It is the kind of music that sounds different in the morning than it does at night, and reveals more with each listen.
